Algorithms

This page explains, end to end, how PlasticIceSheet.jl turns a margin, a bed, and a basal-shear-stress field into an ice sheet — and how it differentiates the result with respect to the shear stress.

1. The governing equation

The reconstruction assumes perfectly plastic, steady-state ice. In that limit the basal shear stress \tau_b exactly balances the gravitational driving stress \tau_d:

\tau_b = \tau_d . \tag{1}

The driving stress is set by the ice geometry — for an ice surface elevation z_s, bed elevation z_b, and thickness H = z_s - z_b, with \rho_i the ice density and g gravity:

\tau_d = \rho_i\, g\, H\, |\nabla z_s| . \tag{2}

Combining Equation 1 and Equation 2 relates the prescribed basal shear stress to the surface geometry:

\tau_b = \rho_i\, g\, H\, |\nabla z_s| . \tag{3}

In a reconstruction \tau_b is prescribed (it is the field we choose or invert for) and the surface z_s is the unknown. Rearranging for the surface slope gives the equation we actually solve:

|\nabla z_s| = \frac{\tau_b}{\rho_i\, g\, H} = \frac{\tau_b}{\rho_i\, g\, (z_s - z_b)} . \tag{4}

The right-hand side is a slope (dimensionless). Equation 4 is a static Hamilton–Jacobi equation: the surface is built inward from the margin, where H \to 0. Its upwind (viscosity) solution automatically yields the correct envelope wherever ice converges, so ice divides, domes, and merging ice caps appear without any explicit flowline or saddle handling.

This is the same physics as ICESHEET 2.0, but solved on a grid rather than by tracing characteristics.

2. Solve for H^2, not z_s

Near the margin H \to 0, so the driving slope \tau_b/(\rho_i g H) in Equation 4 diverges. Discretising Equation 4 for z_s directly is therefore inaccurate near the margin and biases the whole reconstruction thin. The fix is a change of variable.

On a flat bed, Equation 4 reads |\nabla H| = \tau_b/(\rho_i g H). Multiplying by 2H and using \nabla(H^2) = 2H\,\nabla H,

\bigl|\nabla (H^2)\bigr| = \frac{2\tau_b}{\rho_i\, g}. \tag{5}

The singularity is gone: the working variable w \equiv H^2 satisfies a plain eikonal |\nabla w| = G with a finite right-hand side G = 2\tau_b/(\rho_i g), and the boundary condition w = 0 at the margin is regular. Both solver modes work in w = H^2; the thickness is recovered as H = \sqrt{w} and the surface as z_s = H + z_b.

:flat mode

:flat solves Equation 5 exactly and drapes the result on the bed. It is the cheapest mode and is appropriate whenever bed relief is small compared with ice thickness (continental interiors). ICESHEET’s own sensitivity tests indicate the bed-gradient terms are second-order except in mountainous terrain.

3. :full mode — the bed-gradient correction

Over significant relief the bed slope \nabla z_b enters the flux. Writing \nabla z_s = \nabla H + \nabla z_b in Equation 4 and carrying the algebra through in the w = H^2 variable gives, exactly in one dimension,

|\nabla w| = \sqrt{\,G^2 \;-\; 4\sqrt{w}\,(\nabla z_b \cdot \nabla w)\;-\;4\,w\,|\nabla z_b|^2\,}, \qquad G = \frac{2\tau_b}{\rho_i g}. \tag{6}

Two properties make Equation 6 the right object to solve:

  • when \nabla z_b = 0 it reduces to the flat eikonal Equation 5, so :full and :flat agree exactly on a flat bed;
  • it is written in w = H^2, so it inherits the regular margin behaviour of Equation 5.

The effective right-hand side depends on \nabla w (through the \nabla z_b\cdot\nabla w term), so Equation 6 is not a pure eikonal. It is solved by an outer fixed-point iteration: freeze the right-hand side from the current w, solve the resulting constant-RHS eikonal for a candidate w_c, and update.

Damping is required

The naive update w \leftarrow \text{Eikonal}\!\left(\text{RHS}(w)\right) is not contractive — over real bed relief it oscillates indefinitely. The solver therefore uses an under-relaxed (damped) Picard iteration,

w^{(k+1)} = (1-\alpha)\, w^{(k)} + \alpha\, \text{Eikonal}\!\left(\text{RHS}(w^{(k)})\right),

with relaxation factor \alpha = \texttt{relax} (default 0.5). Damping converges the iteration to the same fixed point — i.e. to a vanishing residual (§6) — which is also what makes the implicit-differentiation gradient (§7) exact. Smaller \alpha is more robust on steep beds but needs more iterations; beds whose relief approaches the ice thickness over a few cells may not converge at all (lower relax, raise n_outer, refine the grid, or fall back to :flat).

4. The Godunov update

The eikonal |\nabla u| = F is discretised cell-by-cell with the first-order Godunov upwind scheme. At a cell with grid spacings \Delta x, \Delta y, let a and b be the smaller of the two neighbour values along each axis:

a = \min(u_{i-1,j},\, u_{i+1,j}), \qquad b = \min(u_{i,j-1},\, u_{i,j+1}).

The updated value is the smallest u satisfying

\left(\frac{(u-a)_+}{\Delta x}\right)^{2} + \left(\frac{(u-b)_+}{\Delta y}\right)^{2} = F^2, \qquad (\cdot)_+ = \max(\cdot, 0).

In practice: try the two-sided quadratic; accept it only if the result exceeds both neighbours (both upwind terms active); otherwise take the smaller one-sided update \min(a + F\,\Delta x,\; b + F\,\Delta y). This is godunov_eikonal, a pure function with no mutation or indexing — which is what lets the autodiff back ends differentiate it directly.

5. Fast sweeping

The discrete eikonal is solved by the fast-sweeping method: Gauss–Seidel updates that keep the monotone minimum,

u_{i,j} \leftarrow \min\!\bigl(u_{i,j},\; \text{Godunov update}\bigr),

applied over the four diagonal traversal orderings (\nearrow, \nwarrow, \swarrow, \searrow) in rotation. Because each ordering captures characteristics flowing in one quadrant of directions, a handful of sweeps converges the whole field in O(N) work, for any geometry. Interior cells start at +\infty; ice-free cells hold their boundary value (§6) so neighbour look-ups only ever read the working array.

6. Boundary conditions

The margin is the interface between mask (grounded ice) and ice-free cells. Ice-free cells carry the Dirichlet value:

  • Land margin (z_b \ge z_{ss}): H = 0, i.e. w = 0.
  • Marine margin (z_b < z_{ss}, with z_{ss} the sea-surface elevation): the grounding line sits at the flotation thickness H_{flt} — the thickness whose weight balances the local water column, H_{flt} = \frac{\rho_w}{\rho_i}\,\bigl(z_{ss} - z_b\bigr), \qquad w = H_{flt}^2, reproducing ICESHEET’s marine grounding-line treatment. The eikonal then grows the ice inward from that thickness. No separate slope-limiting pre-pass is needed: the maximum surface slope is the plastic constraint the eikonal enforces.

7. Differentiating with respect to \tau_b

Because the solver is a pure function of \tau_b, an objective on the output (a misfit to an observed surface, a target volume, …) is differentiable — enabling basal-shear-stress inversion.

Forward mode

For a scalar \tau_b or a low-dimensional parameterization, ForwardDiff differentiates straight through solve. Cost scales with the number of parameters.

Reverse mode (implicit differentiation)

For a full per-cell \tau_b field, differentiating through the sweeps would be wasteful. Instead, the converged field satisfies an optimality condition — the Godunov residual

r_{i,j}(w, \tau_b) = w_{i,j} - \bigl(\text{Godunov update at } (i,j)\bigr) = 0

on ice cells (and w - \text{bc} = 0 off-ice). By the implicit function theorem, for a loss L(w),

\frac{\partial L}{\partial \tau_b} = -\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\left(\frac{\partial r}{\partial w}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial r}{\partial \tau_b},

so the gradient costs a single linear solve with the residual Jacobian, independent of the number of sweeps or outer iterations and scalable to a full field. The forward fast sweep is treated as a black box; only the residual is differentiated (with ForwardDiff internally). This is wired up with ImplicitDifferentiation.jl in a package extension, so any reverse-mode AD (e.g. Zygote) obtains the gradient.

The :flat gradient is exact to machine precision; the :full gradient is exact once the damped forward solve (§3) has driven the residual to \approx 0.

NoteNaN hygiene

\sqrt{w} has an infinite derivative at w=0. Wherever \sqrt{w} is differentiated it is floored at H_{\min}^2, because the unguarded \sqrt{0} derivative produces \infty\cdot 0 = \text{NaN} in the reverse pass (forward mode dodges this by branch selection).

8. Velocity and surface mass balance

The plastic solve returns geometry and nothing else. By taking the perfectly-plastic (n\to\infty) limit it has discarded exactly the rate-dependent information that a rate factor, a velocity, and a surface mass balance (SMB) live in — the shape is rate-independent by construction. Those quantities are not hidden in the solution waiting to be read off; recovering them requires one extra closure layered on the fixed geometry.

The geometry already fixes the flow direction

The plastic balance Equation 3 is the membrane-free limit of the SSA/DIVA momentum balance: with \tau_d=\tau_b and the basal drag opposing flow, the longitudinal-stress-divergence term vanishes. Two consequences follow. First, the velocity is local and analytical — no elliptic solve. Second, ice flows down the surface gradient, along the characteristics of the eikonal, so the direction is known everywhere:

\hat{\mathbf u} = -\,\frac{\nabla z_s}{|\nabla z_s|}. \tag{7}

What the geometry does not fix is the flux magnitude — a plastic bed sets the direction but not the speed. Closing that gap needs one assumption, and there are two dual ways to make it.

Closure B — parameters → velocity → SMB

Assume a rheology and a sliding law. The depth-averaged speed splits into internal deformation and basal sliding, both evaluated at the local driving stress \tau=\tau_b:

|\bar{\mathbf u}| = \underbrace{u_b(\tau_b)}_{\text{sliding law}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{2A}{n+2}\,\tau_b^{\,n}\,H}_{\text{SIA deformation}} . \tag{8}

The deformational term is the SIA closed form with the plastic constraint \tau_d=\tau_b substituted; the sliding term inverts the chosen law for the speed that produces drag \tau_b (basal_velocity). For a regularized-Coulomb bed at its Coulomb cap the sliding speed is genuinely undetermined and is returned as NaN rather than fabricated. With \bar{\mathbf u}=|\bar{\mathbf u}|\,\hat{\mathbf u} in hand, steady-state mass continuity gives the implied SMB as a flux divergence:

\dot b = \nabla\!\cdot\mathbf q = \nabla\!\cdot(\bar{\mathbf u}\,H). \tag{9}

This is diva_velocity followed by smb_from_velocity. Because the velocity is a smooth function of \tau_b, so is the SMB — the whole closure doubles as an observation operator for \tau_b inversion (§7).

Closure A — SMB → velocity

The dual. Prescribe \dot b and route it down the fixed surface to recover the flux the ice must carry to be in steady state — the balance velocity (Budd & Warner 1996):

\nabla\!\cdot\mathbf q = \dot b, \qquad \mathbf q \parallel \hat{\mathbf u}, \qquad |\bar{\mathbf u}| = \frac{|\mathbf q|}{H}. \tag{10}

This is the rigorous reading when \tau_b is a basal sliding resistance, where the speed is non-local — set by upstream accumulation, not local rheology. It is balance_flux / balance_velocity.

One discrete flux operator

Closures A and B are exact inverses in the continuum, and the implementation preserves that discretely. Both are built on a single finite-volume multiple-flow-direction (MFD) operator: a cell’s throughput is partitioned to its lower grounded neighbours in proportion to their surface drop. The routing (balance_flux, source → throughput) and the implied-SMB divergence (smb_from_velocity, throughput → source) use the same weights, so they are adjoint and

\texttt{balance\_flux}\bigl(\texttt{smb\_from\_velocity}(\bar{\mathbf u})\bigr) = \bar{\mathbf u}\,H

holds to machine precision. Routing processes cells from highest surface to lowest, so every upstream contribution has arrived before a cell distributes its throughput; flux delivered to the margin terminates there (the catchment discharge), and mass is conserved exactly. This consistency is not cosmetic — it is what makes the SMB-driven reconstruction (§9) have a clean fixed point.

Consistency: where A and B meet

Subtracting the analytical deformation Equation 8 from the balance speed Equation 10 leaves the basal velocity the geometry and SMB jointly demand,

u_{\text{basal}} = u_{\text{balance}} - u_d, \tag{11}

(implied_basal_velocity). Inverting a sliding law at this u_{\text{basal}} and the local \tau_b gives the bed friction implied by the reconstruction — a check on whether the plastic geometry plus an assumed climate is consistent with a plausible bed. A negative value flags a cell where the balance flux is smaller than internal deformation alone would carry.

9. SMB-driven reconstruction

Closures A and B read quantities off a geometry. Their machinery also supports the dual of solve itself: reconstruct the geometry driven by an SMB instead of a prescribed yield stress, on the same prescribed margin (diva_reconstruct). This is the plastic solver with \tau_b no longer prescribed but derived each iteration:

  1. route \dot b down the current surface for the required throughput \mathbf q (§8);
  2. invert the DIVA flux law Equation 8 pointwise for the driving stress \tau_d that carries it, |\mathbf q| = H\bigl(u_b(\tau_d) + \tfrac{2A}{n+2}\tau_d^{\,n}H\bigr) (monotone in \tau_d, solved by bisection);
  3. sweep the plastic eikonal (§4–5) with that \tau_d field for an updated thickness;
  4. under-relax and repeat.

Because routing is the exact inverse of the implied-SMB divergence (§8), a plastic solution fed its own closure-B SMB is a clean fixed point: step 1 recovers exactly \bar{\mathbf u}H, step 2 returns exactly the original \tau_b, and step 3 reproduces the geometry. The eikonal in step 3 also makes the thickness non-local — the interior, where the local flux-to-thickness map is singular at divides, is set by integrating inward from the margin rather than cell by cell.

Like the :full outer iteration (§3), the “route, re-invert, re-sweep” map is not contractive and must be under-relaxed (Equation 6’s damping, here with default \alpha=\texttt{relax}=0.05). Stiffer or finer grids trade a smaller \alpha for more iterations.

This is the lighter middle path between the plastic reconstruction (margin and yield stress in, geometry out; SMB-agnostic) and a full thermomechanical model: it stays a plastic-style geometry solver but is driven by climate. A complete free-margin, nonlinear-diffusion steady state is deliberately not attempted here — that is the job of a dynamic model such as Yelmo.

10. What was deliberately dropped

Relative to ICESHEET 2.0, the grid Hamilton–Jacobi solver makes the following machinery unnecessary: method-of-characteristics flowline tracing, crossover detection (the motorcycle-graph algorithm), saddle/dome detection and polygon splitting, the GIA / sea-level iteration loop, and the GMT projection/preprocessing pipeline. See the Design notes for the full accounting.
