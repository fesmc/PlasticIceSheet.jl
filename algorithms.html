Algorithms

This page explains, end to end, how PlasticIceSheet.jl turns a margin, a bed, and a basal-shear-stress field into an ice sheet — and how it differentiates the result with respect to the shear stress.

1. The governing equation

The reconstruction assumes perfectly plastic, steady-state ice: the gravitational driving stress is everywhere balanced by the basal shear stress \tau. For an ice surface E, bed B, and thickness H = E - B, this balance is

|\nabla E| = \frac{\tau}{\rho_i\, g\, (E - B)} = \frac{\tau}{\rho_i\, g\, H}, \tag{1}

with \rho_i the ice density and g gravity. The right-hand side is a slope (dimensionless). Equation 1 is a static Hamilton–Jacobi equation: the surface is built inward from the margin, where H \to 0. Its upwind (viscosity) solution automatically yields the correct envelope wherever ice converges, so ice divides, domes, and merging ice caps appear without any explicit flowline or saddle handling.

This is the same physics as ICESHEET 1.0, but solved on a grid rather than by tracing characteristics.

2. Solve for H^2, not E

Near the margin H \to 0, so the driving slope \tau/(\rho_i g H) in Equation 1 diverges. Discretising Equation 1 for E directly is therefore inaccurate near the margin and biases the whole reconstruction thin. The fix is a change of variable.

On a flat bed, Equation 1 reads |\nabla H| = \tau/(\rho_i g H). Multiplying by 2H and using \nabla(H^2) = 2H\,\nabla H,

\bigl|\nabla (H^2)\bigr| = \frac{2\tau}{\rho_i\, g}. \tag{2}

The singularity is gone: w \equiv H^2 satisfies a plain eikonal |\nabla w| = G with a finite right-hand side G = 2\tau/(\rho_i g), and the boundary condition w = 0 at the margin is regular. Both solver modes work in w = H^2; the thickness is recovered as H = \sqrt{w} and the surface as E = H + B.

:flat mode

:flat solves Equation 2 exactly and drapes the result on the bed. It is the cheapest mode and is appropriate whenever bed relief is small compared with ice thickness (continental interiors). ICESHEET’s own sensitivity tests indicate the bed-gradient terms are second-order except in mountainous terrain.

3. :hj mode — the bed-gradient correction

Over significant relief the bed slope \nabla B enters the flux. Writing \nabla E = \nabla H + \nabla B in Equation 1 and carrying the algebra through in the w = H^2 variable gives, exactly in one dimension,

|\nabla w| = \sqrt{\,G^2 \;-\; 4\sqrt{w}\,(\nabla B \cdot \nabla w)\;-\;4\,w\,|\nabla B|^2\,}, \qquad G = \frac{2\tau}{\rho_i g}. \tag{3}

Two properties make Equation 3 the right object to solve:

  • when \nabla B = 0 it reduces to the flat eikonal Equation 2, so :hj and :flat agree exactly on a flat bed;
  • it is written in w = H^2, so it inherits the regular margin behaviour of Equation 2.

The effective right-hand side depends on \nabla w (through the \nabla B\cdot\nabla w term), so Equation 3 is not a pure eikonal. It is solved by an outer fixed-point iteration: freeze the right-hand side from the current w, solve the resulting constant-RHS eikonal for a candidate w_c, and update.

Damping is required

The naive update w \leftarrow \text{Eikonal}\!\left(\text{RHS}(w)\right) is not contractive — over real bed relief it oscillates indefinitely. The solver therefore uses an under-relaxed (damped) Picard iteration,

w^{(k+1)} = (1-\alpha)\, w^{(k)} + \alpha\, \text{Eikonal}\!\left(\text{RHS}(w^{(k)})\right),

with relaxation factor \alpha = \texttt{relax} (default 0.5). Damping converges the iteration to the same fixed point — i.e. to a vanishing residual (§6) — which is also what makes the implicit-differentiation gradient (§7) exact. Smaller \alpha is more robust on steep beds but needs more iterations; beds whose relief approaches the ice thickness over a few cells may not converge at all (lower relax, raise n_outer, refine the grid, or fall back to :flat).

4. The Godunov update

The eikonal |\nabla u| = F is discretised cell-by-cell with the first-order Godunov upwind scheme. At a cell with grid spacings \Delta x, \Delta y, let a and b be the smaller of the two neighbour values along each axis:

a = \min(u_{i-1,j},\, u_{i+1,j}), \qquad b = \min(u_{i,j-1},\, u_{i,j+1}).

The updated value is the smallest u satisfying

\left(\frac{(u-a)_+}{\Delta x}\right)^{2} + \left(\frac{(u-b)_+}{\Delta y}\right)^{2} = F^2, \qquad (\cdot)_+ = \max(\cdot, 0).

In practice: try the two-sided quadratic; accept it only if the result exceeds both neighbours (both upwind terms active); otherwise take the smaller one-sided update \min(a + F\,\Delta x,\; b + F\,\Delta y). This is godunov_eikonal, a pure function with no mutation or indexing — which is what lets the autodiff back ends differentiate it directly.

5. Fast sweeping

The discrete eikonal is solved by the fast-sweeping method: Gauss–Seidel updates that keep the monotone minimum,

u_{i,j} \leftarrow \min\!\bigl(u_{i,j},\; \text{Godunov update}\bigr),

applied over the four diagonal traversal orderings (\nearrow, \nwarrow, \swarrow, \searrow) in rotation. Because each ordering captures characteristics flowing in one quadrant of directions, a handful of sweeps converges the whole field in O(N) work, for any geometry. Interior cells start at +\infty; ice-free cells hold their boundary value (§6) so neighbour look-ups only ever read the working array.

6. Boundary conditions

The margin is the interface between mask (grounded ice) and ice-free cells. Ice-free cells carry the Dirichlet value:

  • Land margin (B \ge sea level): H = 0, i.e. w = 0.
  • Marine margin (B < sea level): the grounding line sits at the flotation thickness — the thickness whose weight balances the local water column, H_{\text{flot}} = \frac{\rho_w}{\rho_i}\,\bigl(\text{sea level} - B\bigr), \qquad w = H_{\text{flot}}^2, reproducing ICESHEET’s marine grounding-line treatment. The eikonal then grows the ice inward from that thickness. No separate slope-limiting pre-pass is needed: the maximum surface slope is the plastic constraint the eikonal enforces.

7. Differentiating with respect to \tau

Because the solver is a pure function of \tau, an objective on the output (a misfit to an observed surface, a target volume, …) is differentiable — enabling basal-shear-stress inversion.

Forward mode

For a scalar \tau or a low-dimensional parameterization, ForwardDiff differentiates straight through solve. Cost scales with the number of parameters.

Reverse mode (implicit differentiation)

For a full per-cell \tau field, differentiating through the sweeps would be wasteful. Instead, the converged field satisfies an optimality condition — the Godunov residual

r_{i,j}(w, \tau) = w_{i,j} - \bigl(\text{Godunov update at } (i,j)\bigr) = 0

on ice cells (and w - \text{bc} = 0 off-ice). By the implicit function theorem, for a loss L(w),

\frac{\partial L}{\partial \tau} = -\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\left(\frac{\partial r}{\partial w}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial r}{\partial \tau},

so the gradient costs a single linear solve with the residual Jacobian, independent of the number of sweeps or outer iterations and scalable to a full field. The forward fast sweep is treated as a black box; only the residual is differentiated (with ForwardDiff internally). This is wired up with ImplicitDifferentiation.jl in a package extension, so any reverse-mode AD (e.g. Zygote) obtains the gradient.

The :flat gradient is exact to machine precision; the :hj gradient is exact once the damped forward solve (§3) has driven the residual to \approx 0.

NoteNaN hygiene

\sqrt{w} has an infinite derivative at w=0. Wherever \sqrt{w} is differentiated it is floored at H_{\min}^2, because the unguarded \sqrt{0} derivative produces \infty\cdot 0 = \text{NaN} in the reverse pass (forward mode dodges this by branch selection).

8. What was deliberately dropped

Relative to ICESHEET 1.0, the grid Hamilton–Jacobi solver makes the following machinery unnecessary: method-of-characteristics flowline tracing, crossover detection (the motorcycle-graph algorithm), saddle/dome detection and polygon splitting, the GIA / sea-level iteration loop, and the GMT projection/preprocessing pipeline. See the Design notes for the full accounting.
