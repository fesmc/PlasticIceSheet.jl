Inverting basal shear stress

The plastic solve turns a margin, a bed, and a basal-shear-stress field \tau_b into an ice sheet. \tau_b carries essentially all of the reconstruction’s freedom, so the practical question is the inverse one: what \tau_b makes the reconstruction match data? This page covers how that inverse problem is posed, the two parameterizations of \tau_b the examples provide (and how to switch between them), and how this relates to — and automates — the hand-tuned shear-stress domains of ICESHEET (Gowan et al. 2016, 2021).

The gradient machinery itself (implicit differentiation of the converged eikonal) lives in Algorithms §7; here we use it.

The inverse problem

Pick an objective on the reconstruction and minimise it over \tau_b. For present-day ice the natural target is the observed surface, through surface_misfit:

\min_{\tau_b}\; \big\langle\, (z_s(\tau_b) - z_s^{\text{obs}})^2 \,\big\rangle_{\text{mask}}, \qquad z_s(\tau_b) = z_b + H(\tau_b).

differentiable_thickness gives \partial H/\partial\tau_b by reverse-mode AD, so any gradient optimiser (the examples use Adam) drives the misfit down. The grounded mask is the prescribed margin — an ICESHEET input, not something inverted — so the recovered ice extent always equals the mask; only the geometry inside it is fit.

The number of free parameters in \tau_b should match what the target data can resolve. A dense present-day surface constrains a dense field; a sparse paleo target (relative sea level) constrains only a handful of regional values. The two parameterizations below sit at those two ends.

Two parameterizations

The example examples/invert_taub_greenland.jl implements both and switches between them with a single environment variable, METHOD:

julia --project=examples examples/invert_taub_greenland.jl                  # METHOD=tiled (default)
METHOD=percell julia --project=examples examples/invert_taub_greenland.jl   # point-by-point

Everything downstream — the objective, the Adam loop, the outputs — is shared; only the map from optimisation parameters to the \tau_b field differs.

Point-by-point (METHOD=percell)

A full per-cell \tau_b field, regularized to a length scale REG_KM by a Gaussian-blur reparameterization: optimise a raw field \varphi and set

\tau_b = \operatorname{clamp}\big(K_x\,\varphi\,K_y\big),

with K_x, K_y separable 1-D Gaussian smoothers (\sigma = \texttt{REG\_KM}/\Delta x cells). Being linear, the blur differentiates cleanly under Zygote, and it guarantees \tau_b varies only on scales \gtrsim REG_KM regardless of the data fit. Many effective degrees of freedom; fits a dense observed surface closely (on Greenland GRL-32KM, ~85 m rmse).

Tiled (METHOD=tiled, default)

A low-dimensional, paleo-transferable parameterization — the automatic version of ICESHEET’s hand-tuned shear-stress domains. Cells are grouped into tiles defined only from data available for an unknown ice sheet: bedrock roughness (windowed standard deviation of z_b, binned into N_ROUGH quantile classes) crossed with a sediment mask (z_sed, split soft/hard at SED_THRESH). The ice thickness and ice-derived drainage basins are never used to draw the tiles. Each tile d carries one scalar \theta_d:

\tau_b(\mathbf x) = \operatorname{clamp}\Big(\textstyle\sum_d \theta_d\, \mathbb 1[\,\mathbf x \in d\,]\Big),

embedded via a one-hot selector matrix (linear, hence Zygote-friendly). The default is N_ROUGH = 4 × 2 sediment classes = 8 tiles. The observed surface enters only as the calibration target — exactly the modern-surface calibration Gowan used for Greenland and Antarctica, and the slot a relative-sea-level objective would occupy for a paleo reconstruction.

Relation to ICESHEET / Gowan et al.

ICESHEET (Gowan et al. 2016) is the same perfectly-plastic, equilibrium forward model. In PaleoMIST (Gowan et al. 2021) \tau_b was chosen by:

  • Target. Relative sea level through a GIA model for the paleo ice sheets (adjust \tau_b to maximise LGM volume, reduce it through deglaciation to fit postglacial sea-level indicators), plus geological margins; and the modern surface for Greenland and Antarctica.
  • Domains. Defined by topographic variability (mountainous vs flat) refined by surficial geology (continuous sediment → low \tau_b; hard/discontinuous → high), with boundaries on fjords, mountain ranges, and the flat interior.
  • Tuning. Manual and iterative — repeated by hand until the fit was acceptable.

The tiled method reproduces this logic but automates both halves: the domains come from bed roughness + sediment programmatically, and the per-domain values come from gradient descent rather than hand-tuning. On Greenland it recovers exactly Gowan’s premise — soft- sediment tiles collapse to 5–32 kPa, hard-bed tiles sit at 60–130 kPa.

How many tiles? A resolution test

The one judgement ICESHEET left to intuition — how many domains — can be made quantitative. examples/invert_taub_sweep_greenland.jl sweeps the tile count and plots surface-misfit rmse against the number of free parameters; the knee of that curve is the data-supported resolution. On Greenland GRL-32KM:

  • Sediment geology dominates, not roughness count. A single hard/soft split (2 tiles) drops rmse from 514 → 356 m — more than eight roughness-only tiles achieve (446 m).
  • The knee is early — ~6–8 tiles. Beyond it the curve flattens (6 tiles → 333 m, 15 tiles → 327 m): extra parameters do not earn their keep.
  • An irreducible floor. Even the best tiling (~327 m) sits well above the per-cell fit (~85 m), so a substantial part of the surface signal reflects \tau_b variability that static bed roughness and sediment cannot explain (basal thermal/hydrological state).

Conditioning note: :flat vs :full

The inversions use mode = :flat. The :full adjoint forms a dense Jacobian over the whole grid, whose direct linear solve becomes singular/ill-posed on large grids; :flat gives a well-conditioned eikonal adjoint and ties \tau_b directly to surface slope, which is the more natural object when fitting a surface. For finer grids the per-cell gradient cost is dominated by that dense solve — see the differentiable_thickness docstring for the iterative-solver option.

Toward a paleo (RSL) reconstruction

The tiled parameterization is the bridge to a genuine paleo reconstruction: keep the bed-derived tiles, and replace the observed-surface objective with relative sea level routed through a GIA model. Because the tiles need no ice-sheet knowledge to define, the same setup transfers to an ice sheet whose geometry is unknown — which is exactly the regime ICESHEET was built for, and where reducing the parameter count is not optional but required.
